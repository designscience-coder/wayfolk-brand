01 — Thesis

Three pillars, one identity

WayFolk's visual identity stands on three principles: editorial warmth, structural logic, and typographic intimacy. Together they create a brand that feels personal, precise, and alive.

Editorial Warmth

Warm neutrals over cold blacks. Earth-tone accents. Photography as primary content — candid, eye-level, everyday intimacy. Editorial restraint. Space and breath. Wabi-sabi imperfection.

Structural Logic

Monochrome-first palette. AA compliance baked in. Two typographic modes — expressive and functional. Flat bordered cards, tight grids, monospace tokens. System over decoration.

Typographic Intimacy

The sans/serif italic interplay — rational claim in sans-serif, emotional payoff in serif italic. Sandstone warmth as canvas. Golden-hour photography. Pill buttons. Two-register design: warm brand layer, precise product layer.

The Governing Idea

WayFolk makes travel personal — through people, not geography. The visual language should feel like opening a letter from a friend who knows the city you're heading to. Every headline starts with what you know and lands on what you feel.

Usage Rules

The Rule

Warm neutrals carry 90% of the interface. Accent colors appear only when something meaningful happens — a connection, a trip, a notification, a place on the globe. Color is earned, not assumed.

Light on Light

Parchment or cream as the canvas. Sand as divider. Accent-light as a tinted background with espresso or charcoal text. Keep it quiet — like natural light on linen.

Dark on Dark

Night as background, cream or sand as text, accent-mid for color moments. The globe view lives here — warm, not cold. Connection arcs glow.

03 — Typography

The interplay — where precision meets warmth

WayFolk's typographic signature: headlines begin in Work Sans Light (the geometric, structural voice) and land on Editorial New Ultralight Italic (the humanist, emotional payoff). Sans meets serif — one clean and geometric, the other warm and organic. IBM Plex Sans handles all body text. IBM Plex Mono for labels and tokens. The interplay is what makes WayFolk feel like neither a tech app nor a travel magazine — but something in between.

04 — Graphic Language

Making travel human

The graphic language splits into two registers: a warm, editorial brand layer for web, marketing, and empty states, and a precise, functional product layer for the UI. The brand breathes warmth. The product earns trust through clarity. The two never blur.

Photography Direction

Candid, golden-hour, warm tones. Human interactions — not landmarks. Shot at eye level, natural light, shallow depth of field. The kind of photo your friend would text you from Lisbon. People in context, not posing. Hands in motion. Slight grain welcome.

café table cobblestone dusk hands on a map morning doorway laughing over food golden hour

Hand-Drawn Illustration

Loose, single-weight line drawings — quick sketch energy. Always warm, never clinical. Used in marketing, social, and app empty states — never in the functional product UI. The line is the brand's handwriting.

coffee cups bicycles doorways people at tables hands + phones luggage tags

Two-Register Principle

Warm brand layer for marketing and onboarding: photography, illustration, Editorial New italic, connection arcs. Precise product layer for the UI itself: Work Sans Medium, clean layout, monochrome-first, functional color only when meaningful.

Buttons & Interaction

Pill-shaped buttons (border-radius: 999px) for brand/marketing contexts. Subtly rounded rectangles for product UI. Outline buttons as secondary actions. The pill is warm; the rectangle is functional.

Connection Arcs — The One Geographic Element

Softened, hand-drawn-feeling arcs between people across cities. They appear in the brand layer and occasionally in the product. The arc represents a relationship between people — not a flight path. Thin, warm-hued, slightly imperfect. More like a line drawn between pins on a corkboard than a vector on a map.

06 — Voice ↔ Visual Alignment

How the brand voice maps to visuals

The approved brand voice defines WayFolk as warm, conversational, people-centered, and never showy. The interplay makes the voice typographic — Work Sans is the rational friend, Editorial New italic is the emotional one.

"warm but not showy"

Work Sans Light is clean without being cold. Editorial New italic adds warmth without decorating. Muted earth tones avoid loudness. Monochrome-first means color is earned. Confident without flashy.

"conversational, not corporate"

The interplay gives headlines a human cadence — like someone thinking aloud. The sans sets up the thought, the serif lands it. Pill buttons feel approachable. Warm cream canvas feels domestic — linen, not chrome.

"people, not geography"

Photography shows human interaction, not landmarks. The Editorial New italic always emphasizes human words: "Folk," "together," "friends," "personal." Connection arcs represent relationships, not routes. Travel made visible through people.

07 — What Comes Next

Decisions to make in Figma

This direction document is a starting point. The interplay pattern and brand voice are defined. The following decisions need design work, not specification.

01

Wordmark & Logo

Does "WayFolk" use the interplay? Work Sans "Way" + Editorial New italic "Folk"? Or a custom wordmark that embodies the principle? Does a symbol sit alongside it?

02

Color Validation

Five hues need testing against real UI — globe on dark mode, notification cards, profile screens. The system is a scaffold, not a final answer.

03

Illustration Style

Commission or create hand-drawn illustrations. Test the single-weight line approach at multiple sizes and on warm surfaces.

04

Photography Brief

Define the shot list. Golden-hour warmth, candid moments, hands in motion. Source or shoot reference images that feel intimate and editorially restrained.

05

Globe Interface

Where color, typography, and graphic language converge. Dark mode, connection density, travel signals. The highest-stakes design surface.

06

Component Library

Pill vs. rounded-rect tokens. Card styles for brand vs. product contexts. Input fields, form patterns, bottom sheets. Build from the warmth of the type and color.
